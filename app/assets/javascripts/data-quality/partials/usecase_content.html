Temperature Sensitivity Use Case

Buildings usually use different amounts of energy depending on how hot or cold it is outside. The Maalka Temperature Sensitivity Tool allows you to create a model that explains how your energy consumption depends on temperature so that you can do things like:
    Predict the end-uses of your building (heating, cooling, baseload)
    Analyze if your building is improving over time
    Identify problems in you building’s performance
For example, cooling systems are often driven by electricity. Therefore, electricity-use is expected to significantly increase in buildings as temperature increases past a certain point (typically 65 F). Understanding at which temperature electricity begins to increase tells us a lot about the building.

What does this tell us?
The plot shows us that between 02/01/2010 and 02/01/2017, our electricity-use is increasing signficantly with each degree increase in temperature starting at 35° F. This continues until 71° F is reached and then remains relatively constant. This is a sample file, but if this was a real building we would have reason to investigate why this increase happens at such a low temperature. Energy of a good building would start increasing at around 70° F, when the building begins to require some conditioning to keep occupants cool.
What else can we do with the tool?
If we click in the Details Box, we can actually see the equation that describes the temperature sensitivity model of our data. This is great because we can use this equation to compare our building’s energy-use over time.
The equation, which is reflected in the plot, provides important information about how our electricity-use is impacted by temperature at different ranges. You can tell that between 35° F and 71° F, electricity-use changes much faster than it does over other ranges. We can now apply this model and equation to compare electricity-use over time.
How can we use this equation?
Let us look at the first part of the equation, which says that for temperatures below 35° F, the predicted electricity-use is 4.33 minus the temperature* 0.0153. For any period in the future, if the temperature is below 35° F, we can plug in that temperature into this part of our equation and get a value for predicted electricity-use based on our buildings current behavior. If we use all the parts of the equation, then we can predict the electricity-use for any temperature. That means we can plot predicted electricity-use over time as in the example below.
In the plot above, we have taken the equation from the Temperature Sensitivity Tool and applied it to monthly electricity-use as it is reported to determine if the building’s performance is improving over time. In this plot, the purple bars represent actual energy value, the blue line represents the predicted energy based on the model equation, and the red and green bars below represent the difference between the actual and predicted energy values. If the building is using more energy than the model predicts based on previous performance, then the difference is displayed as a red bar. In this case, the building is doing worse over time. We can add all the red and green bars to see the overall change over time.
How can we use this analysis?
Whenever a building is changed, like with improved insulation or a new boiler system, it is often desirable to know if the change has had an impact on the building’s energy efficiency. This also applies to measuring progress towards energy-use reduction goals. If your analysis simply measures the change in energy over time, your conclusions might be flawed because the changes might be due to external variables like temperature. For example, if your building is in a very hot climate and your analysis is over an abnormally cool period, you may think that the building’s efficiency has improved when in fact it is only using less energy because the cooling systems have not been needed. The Maalka Temperature Sensitivity Tool generates a model that you can apply to control for temperature in your analysis. That way, you can be confident that changes in energy-use for your building are due to changes in your building’s performance and not from uncontrollable weather patterns.
